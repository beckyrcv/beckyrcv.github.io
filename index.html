Unit 1-1

The Devanagari Script

Overview

Hindi!!a language spoken in India is written left to right using the Devanagri!!a script used to write Hindi and other languages script. The Devanagri script has 33 consonants and 11 vowels. Devanagri letters hang from a horizontal line (called the shirorekha). The shirorekha connects letters belonging to the same word. Hindi has inherited some features from Sanskrit, an older language that is also written with the Devanagri script.

Vowels

Hindi has 11 vowels.Note that ऋ is not a vowel in the traditional sense. Every vowel, except for अ, has both independent and dependent forms. The dependent form is called a maatraa form.

The independent vowel form is used when:

  • the vowel is by itself
  • the vowel starts a word, or
  • the vowel follows another vowel.

The dependent vowel form is used after consonants.

Dependent Vowel Forms

A dependent vowel form in Hindi is like a " decorator". It "decorates" a consonant to indicate that the vowel follows the decorated consonant.

Each Hindi consonant is inherently associated with the अ vowel sound. If a consonant is written by itself without any additional marker, it is assumed that it is followed by the vowel अ. Because of this property, the vowel अ does not have a dependent form. The inherent अ sound is "canceled-out" (i.e. replaced) by another vowel's dependent form or if the halant sign is attached to the consonant.

Vowel Nasalization

All vowels, except for ऋ, can be nasalized. Nasalization means pronouncing the vowel with some air flowing through the nose. In Hindi, nasalization is indicated with a mark above the independent or dependent vowel sign. This can either be a dot (ं, bindu) or dot with a crescent shape underneath (ँ, candrabindu). If the vowel has a part that goes above the horizontal line (shirorekha), the bindu is typically used. Otherwise the candrabindu is typically used. For example, ए is nasalized like this: एँ while ऐ is nasalized like this ऐं.

Consonants

Consonant Types

Hindi consonants are typically presented by type on the basis of mouth position and airflow.

There are 5 primary groupings based on mouth position:

  1. Velar
  2. Palatal
  3. Retroflex
  4. Dental, and
  5. Labial

In the velar position, the toungue touches the soft palate.

In the palatal position, the toungue touches the hard palate.

In the retroflex position, the toungue is placed up curving slightly backwards and touches the front of the hard palate.

In the dental position, the toungue tip touches back of the front teeth.

Finally, in the labial position, the sound is made using the lips.

Airflow is primarily grouped by:

  1. Voiced/Unvoiced
  2. Aspirated/Unaspirated, and
  3. Nasal

Voiced consonants are pronounced using the vocal cords (they vibrate).

Unvoiced consonants are pronounced without using the vocal cords (they don't vibrate).

Aspirated consonants are pronounced with lots of air after the consonant.

Unaspirated consonants are pronounced with no air after the consonant.

Nasal consonants are pronounced with some air passing through the nose.

Additional consonants are part of the semivowel, sibilant, and glottal stop categories.

Devanagari Script: Additional Symbols

The ः symbol (visarga) is used to pronounce a sound similar to ह for words from Sanskrit.

Devanagari Conjunct Consonants

Since each consonant has an inherent vowel sound included, Hindi has two ways to indicate consonent clusters.

  1. The ् (halant sign) is used to show that there is no vowel sound with a certain consonent.
  2. Consonant symbols are joined together to show that there is no intermediate vowel.

Devanagari Punctuation

A । (viraam)is used at the end of a sentence as a period would be used in English. Otherwise, punctuation is the same as English.

Unit 1-2

Nouns

Nouns refer to people, places, or things. Hindi nouns can change formThis is called to show information about the noun.Nouns are not the only elements that change like this. The form of a noun indicates two important noun properties: its number and role in the sentence.

Noun form changes follow two general patterns. These two patterns are called "masculine" and "feminine". The classification itself is called grammatical gender. For living things, although there can be some similarity between the gender of a noun and its grammatical gender, the correspondence between gender and grammatical gender is not one-to-one.

Additionally, grammatical gender is important because other words that go together with nouns will also change form to show the connection. Changing one word to show a connection to another word is called agreement. Because agreement happens, it is important to know the grammatical gender for every noun.

Each of the two broad noun categories (masculine and feminine) can be subdivided into two categories: marked and unmarked. Marked nouns have an ending that shows whether it is masculine and feminine in the most basic (or "dictionary") form. The endings of unmarked nouns can vary.

Masculine Nouns

Marked Masculine Nouns

Masculine Noun Marker

The last letter of marked masculine nouns is आ (the maatraa form is ा). Examples are: कमरा, लड़का, तोता, केला, जूता, and बेटा.

Marked Masculine Nouns: Singular and Plural
Singular Plural

Unmarked Masculine Nouns

Unmarked Masculine Noun Endings

Unmarked masculine nouns do not end with आ but follow masculine rules for form changes and agreement. Examples are: घर, कागज़, फल, शहर, जानवर, and आदमी.

Irregular Masculine Noun Endings

Feminine Nouns

Feminine Nouns belong to one of two categories: marked and unmarked.

Marked Feminine Nouns

Marked feminine nouns end with a ई. Examples are: कुरसी, लड़की, गाड़ी, साड़ी, and मिठाी.

Marked Feminine Nouns: Singular and Plural
Singular Plural
एक मिठाई one sweet तीन मिठाइयाँ five sweets

Unmarked Feminine Nouns

Unmarked feminine nouns end with letter other than ई. Examples are: किताब, मेज़, औरत, भाषा, and माता.
